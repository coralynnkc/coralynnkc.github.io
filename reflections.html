AI & Labor

June 30, 2026 · America Is Headed Toward the Infinite Workweek and This Is Why You're Drowning in Busywork.

Most days now, a noticeable chunk of the job is reading code you did not write. You describe what you want, a few hundred lines materialize in seconds, and then the real shift begins: you stop being the person who produces the code and become the person who interrogates it. The diff looks plausible, which is exactly the problem, because 'plausible' is the only thing the model is optimizing for. It does not reason about your codebase. It predicts what a correct-looking answer would resemble, so its output looks right far more often than it is right, and the gap only shows up once you go looking for it.

The promise was less work. What you actually get is more work in less time. The real work was never the typing. It was the deciding: does this handle the empty case, does it match how the rest of the monolith does it, is this the abstraction you want or just the one that compiles. A model can produce a plausible change in seconds; what it cannot do is hold the whole system in its head and notice that the 'fix' introduces a race condition under the exact load pattern you see during a fundraising surge. That noticing is judgment, and judgment is the scarce thing. The keystrokes are gone, but the responsibility stayed exactly where it was, and responsibility is the one part that does not delegate: you can hand off the typing but not the answer for it.

The cleanest way to describe the change is that the job moved from writing to reading, from author to editor, and reading is the stranger rhythm to live in for eight hours. When you author a function, you have to mean every line, and that act builds a model of why it works. When you read a draft you didn't write, you have to reconstruct that model backwards, under time pressure, with every incentive to assume it's fine. There's no satisfying click of having built something, only the quieter accumulation of having approved, rejected, re-prompted, and cleaned up.

None of it is a new story. Washing machines didn't end up doing your laundry for you. ATMs were supposed to wipe out bank tellers, and banks ended up employing more of them, just doing different work. Calculators didn't make you stop doing math, and the internet didn't make you read less, it made you read constantly. The tool changes where the hard part lives; the hard part doesn't go away. AI does the same thing, faster. When a draft becomes nearly free you don't make one draft, you make ten, and now someone has to read all ten and find the one that quietly made something up. People are starting to name the particular tiredness in that, and they're right to, because generating is fast and a little thrilling while verifying is slow and gives you none of the flow state and all of the responsibility. You can be busy the whole day and feel like you produced nothing, because in a sense you didn't: you supervised.

The work that survives all of this is the work that was arguably always the point. Knowing what good looks like is what keeps a codebase from rotting into something nobody can safely change. Deciding what's worth building is the difference between shipping value and shipping features nobody asked for. Owning the call is what lets a team move fast without breaking the things that actually matter, like the donation page during a surge. None of these get easier when generation gets cheap; if anything they get harder, because there is more plausible output to judge and less time to judge it in. This kind of work is badly undervalued right now, precisely because it doesn't look like work the way typing for eight hours did.

I'm new enough to this that I won't pretend to know how it resolves. Maybe this is the better version of the job, or maybe my optimism is just inexperience, and the exhaustion catches up with me once I've been doing this longer. What I do know is that the part of the work with nowhere left to hide is the part worth getting good at, and that's where I'm trying to put my attention while the rest of it sorts itself out.

Lessons I learned from overhauling the NLP Suite

June 26, 2026 · Porting the NLP Suite from a desktop app to the web.

I spent a few weeks working on turning the NLP Suite, about 100,000 lines of Python tied to a tkinter GUI, into a containerized web app. I went in thinking the hard part would be bug fixes on the back-end, editing decades-old Python. Almost everything I actually learned was about the parts around it.

  • Architectural decisions are much more tangled than I expected. Almost every choice had three or four real alternatives that interacted with each other: tkinter vs. customtkinter vs. leaving the desktop GUI behind entirely, Docker vs. conda vs. a native bundle, one big container vs. several services. 'Full-stack communication' turned out to be its own hard problem, since getting a browser, a Django UI, a FastAPI agent, and two Java services to actually talk to each other took more thought than any single analysis function. What helped was being honest about which decisions were basically forced by constraints and which ones were genuinely arguable.
  • Hygiene is much cheaper to build in than to bolt on later. CI, linting, and unit tests to catch regressions are easy to skip when you're moving fast. I learned why you shouldn't the hard way: the codebase predated all of it, so the first time I pointed a type checker at it I got around a thousand findings. Fixing that all at once is impossible, so I had to ratchet it in file by file. If the rigor had been there from the start it would have been close to free, and instead retrofitting it surfaced a lot of avoidable bugs. The lesson I'm taking forward is to write the test and turn on the linter while I'm writing the code, not after.
  • This matters more with AI, not less. It's easy to one-shot something that works once, and much harder to keep it working over time, and AI makes the first part almost trivial. That made it tempting to skip the structure that keeps a thing maintainable. The gap between 'it ran' and 'I can safely change it three months from now' is exactly where the tests, the CI, and a clear architecture earn their keep.
  • Frontend work is real and high-leverage. I used to think of the interface as decoration sitting on top of the 'real' logic. Porting this changed my mind. The UI is the first and last thing a user touches, and it's what decides whether they trust the tool enough to use it at all. The analysis code could be perfect and still go unused behind a confusing screen, so hand-building the Django forms with some care wasn't extra polish; from the user's side it basically is the project. I still have a lot to learn and read about UI design, but this was a nice start.
  • Document everything, especially now. The reason a decision was made disappears fast, and AI-generated code makes this worse because you can produce a lot of plausible code without ever recording why any of it exists. Two ideas I found useful: the notion of intent debt, the growing gap between what your code does and what you actually meant it to do, and the rule that comments should explain why, not what. So I started leaving the why in comments and a tech-debt doc, like the desktop-only features I switched off on purpose and noted, so the next person knows exactly where the seam is. The next person, of course, is me, three months from now, having forgotten every reason I had.

Gratification is good, actually!

June 23, 2026 · The Cult of Delayed Gratification Is a Lie.

I've been told to delay gratification for as long as I can remember: don't buy the nice thing for yourself now & save for retirement, finish the homework before the fun part, the second marshmallow is always worth more than the first. Atomic Habits, which is more or less how I try to operate, runs on the same premise: a one percent improvement repeated daily is what separates where you end up from where you started. The problem is that it only works if you can stay motivated, and motivation is hard to hold onto when the payoff is far away and you can't cleanly trace it back to any one choice you made to get there (which, to his credit, Clear explicitly acknowledges).

Engineering is one of the few careers that seems to reflect the opposite. The code compiles or it doesn't; CI passes or it fails; the change I made is live in prod or sitting on a dashboard by the end of the day. There's a tight, immediate loop between doing a thing and finding out whether it worked, and the small jolt of a green checkmark is real even when the larger project is months from finished. I think a lot of why I like the work is that it refuses to defer all its rewards into some distant payoff: you get to feel something land now, repeatedly, on the way to the thing that takes a long time.

Importantly, the two aren't actually mutually exclusive. The cult of delayed gratification treats the immediate kind as something close to sinful: a small pleasure you're supposed to trade away for a bigger one later. But the squish and the second marshmallow aren't in competition. The green checkmark doesn't come at the expense of the months-long project; it's the thing that keeps you at the desk long enough for the slow part to pay off at all. I still believe the big things get built gradually, but I've stopped filing the small, immediate gratification of doing one thing well under suspicion. It isn't a tax on the long game, it's something to celebrate.

AI & the Experience Machine

June 18, 2026 · Using AI to predict the World Cup winner.

I don't follow sports closely, but this arrived in my inbox this morning and made me a little sad. Twenty thousand simulations to predict the World Cup winner: the modeling is clever, and I'd happily read the writeup, but it honestly left me asking 'why?' The experience, joy, whimsy that comes from making sports bets (so I've heard); we've taken the things that feel most distinctly human and quietly turned them into a statistical modeling problem. It reminded me of this Wordle solver: I do the NYT suite every morning, and I'm genuinely confused.

The broader pattern, I think, that we're seeing in the world is that AI has capacity to replace laborious, menial tasks, but it shouldn't replace doing what makes life enjoyable. A lyric or instrumental can hold a feeling I could never put into words, yet AI music is uniformly horrendous. Surrounding myself with people I like is not something that will ever depreciate in value. I'm an amateur chess enjoyer, and Stockfish has been stronger than any human for years without making the game obsolete or less worth playing. In the immortal words of Beth Harmon, 'chess isn't always competitive; chess can also be beautiful.'

Human experiences & memories are beautiful in ways that can't be artificially captured. As AI becomes more central to how people live, the distinction worth holding onto is whether it's functioning as a tool to enhance people's lives or as a substitute for living itself.

One side tangent I keep circling back to, even without a clean conclusion: something AI seems to struggle with most is something that feels deeply human and experiential. Memory, specifically: context windows cap what a model can hold; tools like Obsidian and retrieval systems compensate at the edges, but the model still drops the through-line as scope extends, and it degrades in a way a person's memory never would: we forget gradually and fill the gaps, while a model loses whatever falls outside its window. My professor is staunchly opposed to the idea humans work the way LLMs do, just predicting what our next word should be. We're more complex than that, built from years of unique experiences, expertise and perspectives that AI simply can't replicate. I'll probably think through this more in a future post.

Nonviolent Communication

June 17, 2026 · Marshall Rosenberg's nonviolent communication.

My manager sent me Marshall Rosenberg's Nonviolent Communication last year, which I dismissed as corporate speak, but it gave me a language for expressing dissatisfaction without it landing as an accusation. As a people-pleaser, I tend to absorb frustration rather than name it, neither giving nor receiving criticism particularly effectively. Rosenberg's argument is that most of what comes out as a judgment is actually a 'tragic expression of an unmet need': the snap or the accusation is a signal about what someone needs, not an attack. NVC gives you a structure for that (observation, feeling, need, request), a way to say what you mean rather than just how you feel, and to turn what would be a demand into a request.

It also made me better at receiving criticism, parsing it into the above categories and recognizing that it comes from a deeply human place. I've always had a negative visceral reaction to criticism (don't we all?) and that's not to say I don't still take things personally, but I think I now have a greater appreciation for criticism that comes from a place of care: wanting you to be better and taking the time to work through that with you. Reading Crying in H Mart recently, one line struck me: "There was no one in the world that was ever as critical or could make me feel as hideous as my mother, but there was no one, not even Peter, who ever made me feel as beautiful."

NVC doesn't resolve that contradiction, but it does give it a name: the criticism and the love were both expressions of the same need, one of them just arrived in a language that didn't make it through. You can't always fix that, but noticing it stops you from treating the translation failure as the whole story.
